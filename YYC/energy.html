Key Observations

Long-Term Convergence

Brent and WTI have tracked closely since 2000, with major divergences during infrastructure bottlenecks. Sharp peaks around 2008 and 2011 reflected global supply shocks from financial crisis recovery and Middle East instability.

Spread Dynamics (2011-2015)

The Brent-WTI spread widened dramatically during the U.S. shale boom due to pipeline constraints from Cushing, OK. This period hurt Canadian producers facing landlocked crude but benefited Calgary-based refiners with access to cheaper WTI.

COVID-19 Collapse (2020)

Global lockdowns drove demand destruction, briefly sending WTI into negative territory. Calgary energy companies faced unprecedented financial stress, leading to bankruptcies, M&A consolidation, and workforce reductions.

Current Stability (2024-2026)

Brent remains slightly above WTI with a stable $3-4 spread, signaling normalized global flows and infrastructure capacity. This predictability benefits Calgary businesses planning capital allocation and hedging strategies.

What This Means for Calgary

Energy Sector Impact: Higher WTI prices boost Calgary's oil and gas sector, driving investment in drilling, production, and infrastructure. Corporate earnings rise, supporting job creation and tax revenue.

Refinery Margins: A narrow Brent-WTI spread reduces arbitrage opportunities but signals healthy global market integration. Calgary refiners benefit from stable input costs and predictable planning horizons.

Geopolitical Risk: Price volatility remains elevated due to Middle East tensions, OPEC+ production decisions, and sanctions. Calgary businesses must maintain robust risk management and scenario planning.

Energy Transition: Long-term trends favor carbon capture, hydrogen, and renewables. Calgary companies investing in clean energy technologies position themselves for future competitiveness as global markets decarbonize.
